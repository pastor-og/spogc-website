The question isn't 'who is my neighbor?' It's 'to whom will I be a neighbor?'

Full sermon · "The Stewardship of Compassion"
Scripture text will be populated from the Bible API or manual entry. Luke 10:25–37
1
The wrong question

The lawyer asks 'who is my neighbor?' hoping for a boundary. Jesus answers with a story that removes all boundaries. The question isn't who qualifies — it's whether you'll stop.

2
Three passersby, one decision

The priest and Levite had reasons. The Samaritan had reasons not to. Compassion isn't the absence of reasons to keep walking — it's stopping anyway.

3
Go and do likewise

Jesus doesn't say 'feel likewise.' He says 'go and do.' Compassion is a verb. Stewardship of compassion means treating your capacity to help as something entrusted to you by God.

3

Go deeper

  • When was the last time you 'crossed to the other side of the road' — avoided someone or something that needed your attention?
  • The Samaritan was the outsider, the unlikely hero. Who are the 'Samaritans' in your community — the people you might not expect compassion from?
  • Compassion cost the Samaritan time, money, and reputation. What is compassion costing you right now? What would it cost if you actually stopped?
  • Is there a need in front of you this week that you've been too busy to see?

For a small group or over coffee:

  • Tell about a time someone showed you unexpected compassion. What did it feel like?
  • Why do you think the priest and the Levite walked past? Can you relate to their reasons?
  • How do you decide when to help and when to keep walking? Is that calculus right?

The Interruption Practice

For three days this week, commit to not ignoring the interruption. When someone needs help — the coworker who needs to talk, the person struggling with bags, the neighbor who looks like they've had a day — stop. Give them five minutes. Don't solve anything. Just be present. At the end of each day, ask: 'God, who did you put in my path today that I almost missed?'
